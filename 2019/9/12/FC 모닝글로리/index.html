Funny Characters

en At first, it was the language written on the street signs and billboards that caught my eye. My interest soon expands to almost all media that language could attach to in form of written characters. Such characters – some are funny, others not – are nevertheless perfect lenses. What language is it? Who write them? What do they want by these characters? Why write like this? So many fascinating insights could be reflected through these characters in every corner.

모닝글로리

Characters

모닝글로리

Commentary

The Hangul here catches my eye, and I think this shop sign is an interesting one. For an average westerner who normally know little about Chinese and Korean but know English to a greater or lesser extent, he will understand what the shop sells through Latin letters “stationery”; for Chinese and Japanese, they can understand through Hanzi/Kanji for the word “文具店” is a shared term both in their vocabulary (WenJuDian/BunGuTen); for Korean people, though they also share this same sinoxenic word written in Hangul as “문구점(MunGuJeom)”, the text on the sign “모닝글로리” has nothing to do with stationery at all, but means a type of flower (a transcription of English word “morning glory”), from which a major Korean stationery brand derived its name. The Hangul here implys that this shop might be run by some Korean ethnicity because it requires a relative high context by only using a brand name to refer the whole product category. Considering that there are many foreign students – of whom Koreans make up a large proportion – living in Wudaokou area, this shop sign send messages in different levels to the crowd, in a pragmatic way. And after some internet searching, I find out that this shop is really owned by Korean ethnicity whose name is “Kim Kiseon (金基善,김기선)”, though the meaning of “萬方” here (and in traditional Chinese) remains unclear to me.
